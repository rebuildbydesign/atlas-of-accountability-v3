PASS 2Voice — one finding per lens

Holding placement constant (companion card, Variant B), here's how the "so what?" reads for each lens using real numbers from Atlas_FEMA_V2.geojson. Voice draft: stat-led, plain language, sourced. These are starting points — pick the ones that land and we'll sharpen.

FEMA Disaster Declarations

SO WHAT?
93% of counties
faced at least one federally declared climate disaster between 2011–2024. Over a quarter (27%) faced six or more.
FEMA / iParametric, 2011–2024 · 2,997 of 3,235 U.S. counties

Why this works: the 93% number reframes climate risk as universal, not regional — that's the editorial through-line of the whole Atlas. The "27% had 6+" second clause prevents readers from dismissing the headline as just "everywhere got rained on once."

FEMA Disaster Funding

SO WHAT?
85¢ of every $1
in FEMA recovery funding (2011–2024) went to just the top 10% of counties. 11% of counties received nothing.
FEMA OpenFEMA · $48.1B obligated nationally · Public Assistance + Hazard Mitigation

Why this works: turns the funding lens from "look at the map" into a concentration story. The 85% / top-10% framing is the kind of stat foundations cite verbatim.

Social Vulnerability (CDC SVI)

SO WHAT?
1.4× more disasters
hit the most-vulnerable U.S. counties (top quintile SVI) compared with the least-vulnerable — 4.9 vs 3.5 declarations on average.
CDC/ATSDR SVI 2022 · cross-tabbed with FEMA declarations 2011–2024

Caveat: 1.4× is real but smaller than the audience may expect. Consider pairing with a sharper sub-cut (e.g. "of the 605 counties with both 25%+ older adults and 6+ disasters, X are top-quintile SVI") if a bigger gap exists in the data.

Energy Reliability (SAIDI)

SO WHAT?
99 hours
— over 4 days — without power per year in the hardest-hit U.S. county. The typical county loses 5.7 hours; the worst 10% lose 16+ hours.
EIA 2022 · SAIDI minutes / customer / year, average across serving utilities

Why this works: turning "5,942 minutes" into "99 hours / 4 days" makes the magnitude legible. The progression (median → 90th-pct → worst) lets the reader place their own county in the distribution.

Urban Counties

SO WHAT?
$39.8B → 292M people
— 83% of all FEMA recovery dollars (2011–2024) flowed to the 31% of counties classified as Urban, where 88% of Americans live.
OMB urban/rural classification · FEMA OpenFEMA 2011–2024

Why this works: sets up the rural comparison on the very next card. Urban headline says "the dollars track the population" — Rural headline reverses that.

Rural Counties

SO WHAT?
$209 vs $137 per person
Rural counties receive more FEMA recovery dollars per capita than urban ones — but the total pot is just $8.2B for 2,148 counties.
OMB urban/rural classification · FEMA per-capita derived from county populations

Counter-narrative: this is the kind of stat that earns trust with skeptical journalists — it pushes back on the simplistic "rural gets shortchanged" frame while still surfacing the absolute-dollar gap.

Older Adults 60+

SO WHAT?
605 counties
have a 25%+ older-adult population and have faced six or more federally declared climate disasters since 2011 — home to millions of aging residents on the front line.
U.S. Census ACS · FEMA declarations 2011–2024 · Older Americans Act threshold (60+)

Why this works: the bivariate "AND" is exactly what this lens visualizes. The finding mirrors the map's logic instead of fighting it.

Recommendation: ship Variant B (companion card) as the default placement. Its hierarchy — eyebrow → stat → interpretation → source — maps to how funders, planners, and journalists read. Variant F (legend header) is the strong runner-up if vertical space stays tight on mobile. Variant E (floating card) is worth revisiting later if you add a "Share this finding" feature.
